# Chapter 1: Arjuna Visada Yoga — Arjuna's Dilemma

## Summary

Chapter 1 of the Bhagavad Gita opens on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, where the armies of the Kauravas and Pandavas have gathered for war. The blind king Dhritarashtra asks his charioteer Sanjaya to describe the scene. Sanjaya relays how Duryodhana, seeing the formidable Pandava army arrayed by Dhrishtadyumna, approaches his teacher Drona and nervously enumerates the great warriors on both sides. Conch shells are blown by the commanders of each army, and the stage is set for battle.

Arjuna asks Krishna, his charioteer, to drive their chariot between the two armies so he can survey those who have come to fight. When Arjuna looks upon the opposing ranks, he recognizes fathers, grandfathers, teachers, uncles, brothers, sons, grandsons, friends, and in-laws stationed on both sides. The sight of his own kinsmen arrayed against one another overwhelms him with compassion and despair. His limbs grow weak, his mouth dries, his body trembles, and the great bow Gandiva slips from his hand.

Arjuna voices his anguish to Krishna, declaring that he sees no good in slaying his own people. He argues that destroying a family leads to the collapse of its dharma, the corruption of its women, the intermingling of social orders, and the ruin of ancestral rites — consequences that condemn the destroyers to hell. He renounces all desire for victory, kingdom, and pleasure, saying he would rather be killed unarmed than commit such a sin.

The chapter concludes with Sanjaya's narration: having spoken these words, Arjuna casts aside his bow and arrows and sinks onto the seat of his chariot, his mind overwhelmed with grief. This moment of crisis — a warrior paralyzed by moral anguish on the eve of battle — sets the stage for Krishna's teachings that unfold across the remaining seventeen chapters.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verse 1.1** — Dhritarashtra's opening question frames the entire Gita as a report from the battlefield of dharma (dharma-kshetre kuru-kshetre).
- **Verses 1.26–1.27** — Arjuna sees beloved relatives on both sides and is seized by profound compassion (kripaya paraya avishta).
- **Verses 1.28–1.30** — Physical symptoms of Arjuna's distress: trembling limbs, dry mouth, burning skin, and a reeling mind.
- **Verses 1.32–1.35** — Arjuna rejects kingdom, pleasure, and even life, declaring he would not kill his kinsmen for dominion over the three worlds.
- **Verses 1.40–1.44** — Arjuna's argument about the destruction of family dharma, the fall of ancestral rites, and the inevitable descent into hell.
- **Verse 1.47** — The pivotal closing image: Arjuna drops his bow and collapses in sorrow, unable to act — the dilemma that will require Krishna's divine counsel to resolve.
