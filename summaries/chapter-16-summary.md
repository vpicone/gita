# Chapter 16: Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga — Yoga through Discerning the Divine and Demoniac Natures

In Chapter 16 of the Bhagavad Gita, Lord Krishna draws a sharp distinction between two fundamental types of human nature: the divine (daivi) and the demoniac (asuri). He begins by enumerating the qualities that belong to those born with a divine disposition — fearlessness, purity of mind, steadfastness in knowledge and yoga, charity, self-control, austerity, truthfulness, non-violence, compassion, forgiveness, fortitude, and humility, among others. These virtues, Krishna explains, lead a person toward spiritual liberation.

Krishna then describes the traits of those born with a demoniac nature: hypocrisy, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness, and ignorance. Such persons do not understand what actions are proper and what should be avoided; they lack purity, right conduct, and truthfulness. They deny any moral foundation to the universe, reject the existence of God, and hold that the world arises from desire alone, without deeper purpose or cause. Driven by insatiable desires, bound by hundreds of hopes, and enslaved by lust and anger, they accumulate wealth through unjust means and remain consumed by ego and delusion.

The chapter vividly portrays the inner monologue of the demoniac mind — "I have gained this today; I will fulfill that desire tomorrow; I am the lord, the enjoyer, the powerful one" — illustrating how unchecked ego and craving trap the soul in an ever-descending cycle of suffering. Krishna warns that those who persist in hatred, cruelty, and envy are cast into demoniac births again and again, sinking further from the divine.

Krishna identifies lust, anger, and greed as the three gates to hell that destroy the self, and urges Arjuna to renounce all three. A person who frees themselves from these destructive impulses works for the genuine welfare of the soul and attains the supreme goal. The chapter closes with Krishna's counsel that scripture should serve as the guide for discerning right from wrong, and that one should act according to scriptural injunctions rather than follow the whims of desire.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verses 16.1-3 (Divine qualities):** A comprehensive list of 26 virtues — including fearlessness, non-violence, truthfulness, compassion, modesty, and absence of vanity — that characterize those destined for liberation.
- **Verse 16.4 (Demoniac qualities):** Hypocrisy, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness, and ignorance mark those bound for bondage.
- **Verse 16.5 (Two destinies):** Divine nature leads to liberation; demoniac nature leads to bondage. Krishna reassures Arjuna that he is born with divine qualities.
- **Verses 16.7-8 (Demoniac worldview):** Those of demoniac nature deny truth, moral order, and God, viewing the universe as purposeless and driven solely by desire.
- **Verse 16.21 (Three gates of hell):** Lust (kama), anger (krodha), and greed (lobha) are identified as the three gateways to self-destruction and must be abandoned.
- **Verse 16.24 (Authority of scripture):** Krishna concludes that scripture should be the authority for determining right action, and one should act accordingly.
