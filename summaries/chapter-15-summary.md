# Chapter 15: Purushottama Yoga -- The Yoga of the Supreme Divine Personality

## Summary

Chapter 15 of the Bhagavad Gita, comprising 20 verses, opens with Krishna describing the material world through the metaphor of an inverted sacred fig tree (ashvattha). This cosmic tree has its roots above in the Supreme and its branches extending downward, with the Vedic hymns as its leaves. Its branches, nourished by the three gunas (modes of nature), spread in all directions, and its secondary roots -- representing karma -- bind beings to the human world. Krishna teaches that the true form of this tree cannot be perceived by ordinary vision, and that one must cut it down with the strong axe of detachment in order to seek the supreme abode from which there is no return.

Krishna then reveals the nature of the individual soul (jiva) as an eternal fragment of the Divine. This soul, bound by material nature, carries the mind and senses from body to body, just as the wind carries fragrances from place to place. Through the senses -- hearing, sight, touch, taste, smell, and mind -- the soul experiences the objects of the world. The ignorant fail to perceive the soul's journey through bodies and its association with the gunas, while those endowed with the eye of knowledge can see it clearly. Even among those who strive, only yogis with purified minds succeed in perceiving the self within.

The chapter further establishes Krishna's role as the sustaining force behind all existence. He declares that the light of the sun, moon, and fire originates from Him. Pervading the earth, He sustains all beings with His energy and, as the moon, nourishes all plant life. As the digestive fire (Vaishvanara), He resides in the bodies of living creatures, working with the vital breaths to digest the four kinds of food. Seated in the hearts of all, He is the source of memory, knowledge, and forgetfulness, and He is the ultimate object of all Vedic knowledge.

In the closing verses, Krishna introduces the doctrine of the three purushas (beings): the perishable (kshara), which includes all created beings; the imperishable (akshara), the unchanging principle; and the Supreme Person (Purushottama), who transcends both. This Supreme Person -- Krishna Himself -- pervades and sustains the three worlds as the indestructible Lord. Because He surpasses both the perishable and the imperishable, He is celebrated in the world and in the Vedas as the Supreme Divine Personality. Krishna concludes by declaring that whoever knows Him without delusion as the Purushottama possesses complete knowledge and worships Him with their whole being, and that this teaching is the most secret of all scriptures.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **The Cosmic Tree (15.1-4):** The inverted ashvattha tree as a metaphor for samsara, to be cut down with the axe of detachment (asanga-shastra).
- **The Eternal Soul (15.7-8):** The jiva as an eternal fragment of the Divine, transmigrating with the mind and senses from body to body.
- **The Eye of Knowledge (15.10-11):** Only those with purified minds and spiritual insight can perceive the soul; the ignorant cannot.
- **Divine Immanence (15.12-15):** Krishna as the light in the sun, moon, and fire; the sustainer of the earth; the digestive fire in all bodies; and the indweller of all hearts from whom come memory, knowledge, and their removal.
- **The Three Purushas (15.16-18):** The perishable, the imperishable, and the Supreme Person (Purushottama) who transcends both.
- **The Most Secret Teaching (15.20):** Understanding this doctrine brings true wisdom and the fulfillment of all duties.
