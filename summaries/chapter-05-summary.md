# Chapter 5: Karma Sanyasa Yoga — Path of Renunciation

Chapter 5 of the Bhagavad Gita opens with Arjuna's direct question to Krishna: which is superior, the renunciation of action (sanyasa) or the disciplined performance of action (karma yoga)? Krishna answers unequivocally that while both paths lead to liberation, karma yoga — selfless action performed without attachment to results — is the better of the two. A true renunciant, Krishna explains, is not someone who merely abandons activity, but one who acts without hatred or desire, free from the pull of dualities such as pleasure and pain.

Krishna goes on to reconcile the seemingly opposed paths by teaching that knowledge (Sankhya) and selfless action (Yoga) ultimately arrive at the same destination. The wise see no difference between them. One who works without personal motive, offering all actions to the Divine and relinquishing attachment, remains untouched by sin — just as a lotus leaf is untouched by water. The body, mind, intellect, and senses may continue to act, but the disciplined yogi acts solely for self-purification, having abandoned the fruit of action.

A central teaching of this chapter is the concept of inner renunciation. Krishna describes the person of steady wisdom as one who mentally renounces all actions, dwelling happily in the "city of nine gates" (the body) — neither acting nor causing others to act in the ultimate sense. The Lord does not create agency or actions for the world; it is nature (svabhava) that acts. The Self neither takes on anyone's sin nor anyone's merit; beings are deluded because their knowledge is covered by ignorance.

Krishna then describes the liberating power of knowledge: when ignorance is destroyed by self-knowledge, that knowledge illuminates the Supreme like the sun. Those whose intellect is established in the Self, who have shaken off sin through knowledge, attain a liberation from which there is no return. The wise look equally upon a learned Brahmin, a cow, an elephant, a dog, and an outcast — seeing the same divine essence in all.

The chapter concludes with a description of the sage who finds happiness, rejoicing, and light within. Such a person, free from desire and anger, with mind and senses controlled, attains liberation (Brahma-nirvana) both in this life and beyond. Krishna introduces the practice of meditation — focusing the gaze, regulating the breath, and withdrawing the senses — as the means to lasting peace, culminating in the recognition that the Lord is the ultimate enjoyer of all sacrifices, the sovereign of all worlds, and the friend of all beings.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verse 5.2** — Karma yoga is superior to renunciation of action.
- **Verse 5.3** — The true renunciant neither hates nor desires and is free from dualities.
- **Verse 5.10** — One who acts without attachment is untouched by sin, as a lotus leaf by water.
- **Verse 5.13** — The "city of nine gates": the embodied soul dwells in the body, mentally renouncing all actions.
- **Verse 5.18** — Equal vision: the wise see the same Self in all beings.
- **Verse 5.29** — Peace comes from knowing Krishna as the enjoyer of sacrifice, lord of all worlds, and friend of all creatures.
