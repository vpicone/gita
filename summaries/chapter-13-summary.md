# Chapter 13: Ksetra Ksetrajna Vibhaaga Yoga — Yoga through Distinguishing the Field and the Knower of the Field

## Summary

Chapter 13 marks a turning point in the Bhagavad Gita, shifting from the themes of devotion explored in earlier chapters to a profound philosophical inquiry into the nature of matter and spirit. Arjuna asks Krishna to explain the distinction between Nature (Prakriti) and Spirit (Purusha), the field of activity and the one who knows it, and what constitutes true knowledge. Krishna responds by defining the body as the "field" (Kshetra) and the conscious Self that witnesses it as the "knower of the field" (Kshetrajna). He further declares that He Himself is the ultimate Knower present in all fields, and that understanding both the field and its knower constitutes genuine knowledge.

Krishna then describes the composition of the field: the five great elements, ego, intellect, the unmanifest, the ten senses, the mind, and the five sense objects, along with desire, aversion, pleasure, pain, consciousness, and will. He enumerates the twenty virtues that constitute true knowledge, including humility, non-violence, forgiveness, simplicity, service to the teacher, purity, steadfastness, self-control, dispassion toward sense objects, absence of egoism, non-attachment, even-mindedness, unwavering devotion, love of solitude, and constancy in self-knowledge. Whatever opposes these qualities is declared to be ignorance.

The chapter then turns to the nature of Brahman, the supreme object of knowledge. Krishna describes Brahman as beginningless, beyond being and non-being, with hands, feet, eyes, and heads everywhere, pervading all yet unattached, without qualities yet experiencing all qualities. Brahman is inside and outside all beings, subtle and unknowable, near and far, undivided yet appearing divided, the sustainer, destroyer, and creator of all beings, and the light beyond darkness seated in the hearts of all.

In the closing verses, Krishna explains the relationship between Prakriti and Purusha. Both are beginningless; all modifications and qualities arise from Nature, while the soul's experience of pleasure and pain comes from its association with Nature's qualities. Attachment to these qualities (gunas) determines the soul's future births. The Supreme Self dwelling in the body is described as the witness, the consenter, the sustainer, and the great Lord. Those who perceive that all actions belong to Nature alone, and that the Self is actionless, truly see. When one recognizes that the diversity of beings rests in the One and proceeds from the One, one attains Brahman.

The chapter concludes with the assurance that those who perceive — through the eye of knowledge — the distinction between the field and its knower, and who understand the liberation of beings from Nature, attain the Supreme.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verse 13.2** — The body is the "field" (Kshetra); the conscious Self is the "knower of the field" (Kshetrajna).
- **Verse 13.3** — Krishna declares Himself the Knower in all fields; knowledge of both field and knower is true knowledge.
- **Verses 13.8-13.12** — The twenty qualities of knowledge: humility, non-violence, dispassion, non-attachment, devotion, and constancy in self-knowledge.
- **Verses 13.13-13.18** — Description of Brahman as the supreme knowable, beyond being and non-being, pervading all, the light of all lights seated in every heart.
- **Verse 13.22** — The soul in Nature experiences the gunas; attachment to qualities causes rebirth.
- **Verse 13.27** — All beings arise from the union of field and knower of the field.
- **Verse 13.30** — True seeing is recognizing that all actions are performed by Nature alone and the Self is actionless.
- **Verse 13.35** — Those who discern the field from its knower, and understand liberation from Nature, attain the Supreme.
