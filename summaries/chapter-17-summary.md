# Chapter 17: Sraddhatraya Vibhaga Yoga -- Yoga through Discerning the Three Divisions of Faith

## Summary

Chapter 17 of the Bhagavad Gita opens with Arjuna asking Krishna about the status of those who worship with faith but disregard scriptural injunctions. Krishna responds by explaining that the faith inherent in every embodied being is threefold, arising from their innate nature (svabhava) and corresponding to the three gunas: sattva (goodness), rajas (passion), and tamas (ignorance). A person's faith defines who they are -- sattvic people worship the gods, rajasic people worship powerful spirits and demons, and tamasic people worship ghosts and the dead. Krishna warns against those who practice severe austerities not sanctioned by scripture, driven by hypocrisy and ego, as they torment both their own bodies and the Divine dwelling within.

Krishna then applies this threefold classification systematically across several domains of human activity. In the matter of food, sattvic foods promote longevity, health, happiness, and strength, and are juicy, nourishing, and agreeable. Rajasic foods are bitter, sour, salty, overly hot, and pungent, producing pain and disease. Tamasic foods are stale, tasteless, putrid, and impure. The same threefold division applies to sacrifice (yajna): sattvic sacrifice is performed as duty without desire for reward, rajasic sacrifice is performed for show or for selfish gain, and tamasic sacrifice is devoid of scriptural authority, faith, and proper offerings.

The chapter gives particular attention to austerity (tapas), which Krishna divides into three kinds by domain -- body, speech, and mind -- before classifying each by guna. Bodily austerity includes worship of the divine and the wise, cleanliness, simplicity, celibacy, and non-violence. Austerity of speech means words that are truthful, pleasant, beneficial, and non-agitating, along with the study of scripture. Mental austerity encompasses serenity, gentleness, silence, self-control, and purity of intention. When these austerities are practiced with supreme faith and without desire for reward, they are sattvic; when performed for recognition and show, they are rajasic; and when undertaken with delusion, self-torture, or intent to harm others, they are tamasic.

Charity (dana) is similarly classified: sattvic charity is given as a matter of duty, to a worthy person, at the right time and place, without expectation of return. Rajasic charity is given reluctantly or with expectation of reward. Tamasic charity is given at the wrong time and place, to the undeserving, and with contempt. The chapter concludes with the sacred formula "Om Tat Sat," the threefold designation of Brahman. "Om" is uttered at the start of all scriptural acts. "Tat" signifies performing acts without attachment to results, seeking liberation. "Sat" denotes reality, goodness, and steadfastness in sacrifice, austerity, and charity. Whatever is done without faith is called "asat" and bears no fruit in this world or the next.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verse 17.3** -- "A person is made of their faith. Whatever their faith is, that is what they become." This verse encapsulates the chapter's central teaching that faith shapes identity and destiny.
- **Verse 17.20** -- Defines sattvic charity as given selflessly, to the right person, at the right place and time, purely from a sense of duty.
- **Verses 17.14-17.16** -- The threefold classification of austerity into body, speech, and mind, providing a practical framework for spiritual discipline.
- **Verses 17.23-17.28** -- The sacred formula "Om Tat Sat" as the triple designation of Brahman, sanctifying all acts of sacrifice, austerity, and charity. Acts performed without faith (ashraddha) are declared "asat" -- without value here or hereafter.
