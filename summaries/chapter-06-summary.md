# Chapter 6: Dhyana Yoga — Path of Meditation

## Summary

Chapter 6 of the Bhagavad Gita opens with Krishna redefining renunciation and yoga as one and the same. A true renunciant (sannyasi) and a true yogi is not someone who abandons fire rituals or ceases all activity, but one who performs obligatory duties without attachment to their fruits. Krishna explains that for a seeker still ascending the path, disciplined action is the means; for one who has already attained yoga, inner stillness and cessation of selfish desire become the means. The mind, he teaches, is both the self's greatest friend and its worst enemy: whoever has conquered the mind finds a faithful ally, while the unconquered mind behaves like a hostile foe.

Krishna then provides detailed, practical instructions for meditation. The yogi should retire to a solitary place, sit on a firm seat that is neither too high nor too low, hold the body erect, and fix the gaze on the tip of the nose. The mind should be serene, fearless, and wholly absorbed in the Divine. Moderation is essential: yoga is not for one who eats too much or too little, nor for one who sleeps excessively or stays awake too long. A balanced life of regulated habits supports the practice. Gradually, through steady intellect and firm resolve, the practitioner should withdraw the mind from sense objects and anchor it in the Self, gently bringing it back each time it wanders. The reward is supreme bliss and the vision of the Self in all beings and all beings in the Self.

Arjuna raises an honest objection: the mind is restless, turbulent, strong, and obstinate — as hard to control as the wind. Krishna acknowledges the difficulty but assures him that the mind can be tamed through persistent practice (abhyasa) and dispassion (vairagya). Arjuna then asks what becomes of someone who begins the path of yoga with faith but fails to reach perfection. Krishna offers a deeply reassuring answer: no effort on this path is ever lost. Such a person is reborn among the virtuous and prosperous, or even rarer, into a family of wise yogis, where they resume their practice from the accumulated merit of previous lives and are drawn forward by the force of past discipline.

The chapter concludes with Krishna's declaration that the yogi surpasses the ascetic, the scholar, and the ritualist. He urges Arjuna to become a yogi. Of all yogis, the one who worships Krishna with full faith and an inner self absorbed in the Divine is regarded as the highest.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verse 6.5** — "One should elevate oneself by one's own mind and not degrade oneself; for the mind alone is one's friend, and the mind alone is one's enemy."
- **Verse 6.17** — Moderation in eating, sleeping, work, and recreation as the foundation of yoga.
- **Verse 6.23** — Yoga defined as the severance of contact with suffering, to be practiced with determination and an undespairing heart.
- **Verse 6.26** — Whenever the restless mind wanders, the practitioner should gently bring it back under the control of the Self.
- **Verse 6.34–35** — Arjuna compares controlling the mind to controlling the wind; Krishna prescribes practice (abhyasa) and detachment (vairagya) as the remedy.
- **Verse 6.40** — No spiritual effort is ever wasted; the unsuccessful yogi is never destroyed.
- **Verse 6.47** — The devotee who worships Krishna with faith and an inner self fixed on the Divine is the greatest of all yogis.
