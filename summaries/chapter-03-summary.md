# Chapter 3: Karma Yoga — Path of Selfless Service

Chapter 3 of the Bhagavad Gita, titled "Karma Yoga" (the Path of Selfless Service), contains 43 verses. It opens with Arjuna's confusion: if Krishna considers knowledge superior to action, why is He urging Arjuna to fight in this terrible battle? Krishna responds by explaining the two paths He has taught since ancient times — the path of knowledge (Jnana Yoga) for the contemplative, and the path of action (Karma Yoga) for the active. He makes clear that no one can attain freedom from action by merely abstaining from work, nor can anyone reach perfection through renunciation alone. Action is unavoidable, for even maintaining the body requires it.

Krishna introduces the central teaching of the chapter: one should perform prescribed duties without attachment to results. He explains the cosmic principle of yajna (sacrifice) — that the world operates through a cycle of mutual sustenance. The gods nourish beings through rain and food, and beings sustain the gods through sacrifice. Those who break this cycle by enjoying resources without contributing live in vain. However, the person who finds satisfaction in the Self alone, delighting in the Self and content in the Self, has no obligatory duty remaining. Such a person has nothing to gain by action or inaction.

Krishna holds up King Janaka and other great figures as examples of those who attained perfection through selfless action. He urges Arjuna to act for the welfare of the world (loka-sangraha), noting that whatever a great person does, common people follow. Krishna Himself, though He has nothing to achieve in the three worlds, continues to act — for if He ceased to act, all beings would follow His example and the world would fall into ruin.

A key teaching concerns the nature of desire and anger. Krishna identifies kama (desire) and krodha (anger), born of the mode of passion (rajo-guna), as the great enemies of the soul. Desire clouds wisdom the way smoke covers fire, dust covers a mirror, or a womb covers an embryo. The senses, mind, and intellect are the seats of desire, and through these it deludes the embodied soul. Krishna concludes the chapter by counseling Arjuna to regulate the senses, steady the mind, and slay this enemy — desire — which is the destroyer of knowledge and self-realization.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **Verse 3.3** — Two paths: Jnana Yoga (knowledge) for the contemplative and Karma Yoga (action) for the active.
- **Verse 3.8** — Perform your prescribed duty, for action is superior to inaction; even the maintenance of the body is impossible without action.
- **Verse 3.9** — Work done as yajna (sacrifice) frees one from bondage; all other work binds.
- **Verse 3.19** — One who acts without attachment attains the Supreme.
- **Verse 3.21** — Whatever a great person does, others follow; whatever standards they set, the world pursues.
- **Verse 3.27** — All actions are performed by the gunas (qualities) of material nature; the ego-deluded soul thinks "I am the doer."
- **Verse 3.37** — Desire and anger, born of rajo-guna, are the great devourers and enemies of the soul.
- **Verse 3.43** — Knowing the Self to be higher than the intellect, steady the mind and slay the enemy in the form of desire.
