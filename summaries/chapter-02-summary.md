# Chapter 2: Sankhya Yoga — Transcendental Knowledge

## Summary

Chapter 2 opens on the battlefield of Kurukshetra with Arjuna overwhelmed by grief and confusion. His eyes filled with tears, he tells Krishna that he cannot bring himself to fight against his own relatives, teachers, and elders. He argues that it would be better to live as a beggar than to enjoy pleasures stained with the blood of his kinsmen. Unable to resolve his moral dilemma, Arjuna surrenders to Krishna as his teacher and asks for guidance.

Krishna begins his instruction by addressing Arjuna's sorrow directly. He teaches that the wise do not grieve for the living or the dead, because the soul (Atman) is eternal, imperishable, and beyond all physical destruction. Bodies are temporary, but the self that inhabits them is never born and never dies. Just as a person discards worn-out garments and puts on new ones, the soul casts off old bodies and enters new ones. This teaching on the immortality of the soul forms the philosophical foundation of the entire Gita.

Having established the nature of the self through Sankhya (analytical knowledge), Krishna then transitions to Karma Yoga, the discipline of selfless action. He delivers one of the most celebrated verses in all of scripture (2.47): "You have a right to perform your prescribed duties, but you are not entitled to the fruits of your actions." Krishna urges Arjuna to act with equanimity, remaining balanced in success and failure, and to perform his duty as a warrior without attachment to outcomes. This evenness of mind, he says, is the true meaning of Yoga.

In the final section, Arjuna asks Krishna to describe the qualities of a person of steady wisdom (sthita-prajna). Krishna explains that such a person has renounced all selfish desires, remains undisturbed by sorrow, is free from attachment, fear, and anger, and finds complete satisfaction within the self. Like an ocean that remains unmoved despite rivers flowing into it, the person of steady wisdom is not shaken by the constant flow of sense impressions. The chapter concludes with Krishna declaring that this state of God-realization (Brahmi-sthiti) is the ultimate goal — one who attains it, even at the hour of death, achieves liberation.

## Key Verses and Concepts

- **2.11** — "The wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead." Krishna's teaching begins with the immortality of the soul.
- **2.22** — The analogy of changing garments: as one discards old clothes for new, the soul passes from body to body.
- **2.47** — "You have the right to work alone, but not to its fruits." The foundational teaching of Karma Yoga and selfless action.
- **2.48** — "Evenness of mind is called Yoga." Equanimity in success and failure defines the yogic attitude.
- **2.55–2.72** — The description of the sthita-prajna (person of steady wisdom), a portrait of the ideal sage who is self-contained, desireless, and established in the Absolute.
